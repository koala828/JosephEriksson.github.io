Unsupervised Learning with Hidden Markov Models

To analyze how individuals adopt and continue using new policies or services over time, we employed a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) in the context of the RUT tax deduction—a Swedish tax subsidy for domestic services.

The diagram highlights how individuals engage with the RUT deduction over time. Non-Adopters have a low probability of starting to use the service, which explains the steady but slow overall adoption rate of the RUT deduction. Once individuals become Adopters, they are more likely to continue using the service, and some may transition into Super-Adopters who use it consistently.

This analysis suggests that the slow adoption of the RUT deduction indicates that adjustments to home and work relative prices are generally slow and but during the project we encountered other reasons that could cause a slow adoption rate aswell.

Analyzing RUT Adoption Through Consumption Trends

To understand the impact of the RUT tax deduction on domestic service consumption in Sweden, we analyzed national accounts data and compared it with neighboring countries and the United States. The following plots illustrate how the increase in RUT usage correlates with domestic service consumption and how Sweden's trends diverge from those of Denmark, Finland, and the US. These insights help assess the effectiveness of the RUT deduction in influencing consumer behavior and the broader economy.

The first plot illustrates that as the total amount claimed under the RUT deduction increased over time, the consumption share of domestic services in Sweden also rose. This suggests a strong correlation between the implementation and expansion of the RUT subsidy and increased spending on domestic services. It indicates that the RUT deduction effectively encouraged households to outsource domestic tasks, shifting from home production to market services.

The second and third plots compare Sweden's consumption trends with those of Denmark and Finland. The top plot shows that Sweden's consumption share of domestic services has increased significantly since the introduction of the RUT deduction, whereas Denmark and Finland have remained relatively stable. This divergence suggests that the rise in Sweden is not part of a broader regional trend but is likely influenced by the RUT policy. The bottom plot shows that consumption trends in restaurants and cafes are similar across all three countries, indicating that the divergence is specific to domestic services and not a general increase in service consumption.

The fourth plot compares Sweden's domestic service consumption with that of the United States, including categories like housekeeping services, gardening, and babysitting. The US data shows no significant upward trend in household service consumption over the same period. This contrast highlights the unique impact of the RUT deduction on Swedish consumer behavior and suggests that without such policy interventions, consumption patterns in domestic services remain relatively flat.

These plots are connected to the project as they provide empirical evidence supporting the hypothesis that policy measures like the RUT deduction can effectively influence consumer behavior and encourage the adoption of market-based domestic services. They illustrate how adjustments to relative prices, facilitated by tax subsidies, can overcome slow adoption rates and shift household consumption patterns. This aligns with the project's focus on analyzing adjustment frictions in the relative prices of home versus market production and their role in the adoption of services like those covered by the RUT deduction.
